# Why You Still Haven't Grown Up — Live Presentation

> Slide = what's on screen (minimal). Say = what you actually say.
> Pause after big lines. Let them land.

---

## SLIDE 1 — Title

**WHY YOU STILL HAVEN'T GROWN UP**

*Say:*
> "I want to start with something that might sting a little.
> There is a reason why intelligent, high-potential people stay stuck.
> And it has nothing to do with their IQ, their access to resources, or their circumstances.
> It has everything to do with one psychological pattern that Jung identified over a hundred years ago.
> And it is more relevant today than it has ever been."

---

## SLIDE 2 — The Concept

**PUER ETERNUS**
*The Eternal Child*

*Say:*
> "Carl Jung described an archetype — a recurring psychological type — called the Puer Eternus.
> Puer means boy. Puella means girl. Eternus means eternal.
> The eternal child.
> This is someone who, no matter how old they get, never really grows up.
> And I don't mean that in the fun, creative, youthful sense.
> I mean they are psychologically stuck in adolescence.
> And what's wild is — these are often the smartest people in the room."

*On slide:*
- Puer = boy / Puella = girl
- Eternus = eternal
- The archetype of someone who never grows up

---

## SLIDE 3 — Who Is the Puer?

**YOU PROBABLY KNOW ONE**

*Say:*
> "Here's what a Puer looks like.
> First — they're charming. Genuinely likable. Often the funniest person in the group.
> Second — they're intelligent. Quick-witted. When you first meet them, you're impressed.
> Third — they have enormous potential. Everyone around them can see it.
> But here's the pattern: they start strong and then fall flat.
> They can't follow through. They can't deliver consistently.
> And the people around them go from impressed... to frustrated.
> Because the potential is real. But it never goes anywhere."

*On slide:*
- Charming, intelligent, likable
- High potential — everyone can see it
- Starts strong → falls flat → repeat

---

## SLIDE 4 — The Fantasy Problem

**REALITY IS ALWAYS DISAPPOINTING**

*Say:*
> "Here is the core issue.
> The Puer has a rich inner fantasy life.
> In their head, they have this vision — the perfect relationship, the perfect career, the perfect life.
> And the moment they engage with reality... reality disappoints.
> The idea of writing a novel is incredible. The actual process of writing is boring and grueling.
> The idea of the perfect partner is everything. The actual person is just a human being with flaws.
> So they retreat. Back to the fantasy. Back to safety.
> And the cycle starts over.
> Marie-Louise von Franz wrote about this over seventy years ago and it's eerie how perfectly it describes what we're seeing today."

*On slide:*
- Fantasy: vivid, perfect, exciting
- Reality: drab, disappointing, flawed
- Response: retreat → back to fantasy → repeat

---

## SLIDE 5 — The Stem Cell

**THE PLURIPOTENT STEM CELL**

*Say:*
> "Here's the metaphor that clicked everything into place for me.
> A pluripotent stem cell can become anything.
> A neuron. A heart cell. A liver cell. Anything.
> But the moment it differentiates — the moment it commits to becoming one thing —
> it loses the potential to be everything else.
> That is the Puer.
> They are swimming in potential.
> And the moment they commit to anything — a major, a career, a relationship —
> they lose all the other options.
> And that is something they simply cannot do.
> Not won't. Cannot.
> The fear of closing off possibilities is pathological."

*On slide:*
- Pluripotent stem cell = can become anything
- Commitment = differentiation = loss of potential
- The Puer cannot make that trade

---

## SLIDE 6 — The Marginal Life

**LIVING IN THE BOARDING AREA**

*Say:*
> "Picture an airport with a hundred gates.
> A hundred planes. A hundred destinations.
> The Puer walks in, sees all the options, and... sits down in the waiting area.
> Because the moment I board one plane, the other 99 are gone.
> So they sit there. For years. Decades sometimes.
> Von Franz called this the provisional life.
> The feeling that you're not yet in your real life.
> That sometime in the future, the real thing will come.
> For now, this is just temporary.
> But temporary becomes permanent.
> And they end up with what psychoanalysts call a failure to constellate —
> individual stars that never form a picture.
> A marginal life. Not a full life. Not a real life."

*On slide:*
- 100 gates, 100 planes — sitting in the boarding area
- The provisional life: "this is just for now"
- Failure to constellate — stars with no picture

---

## SLIDE 7 — The Cycle

**HOW IT ACTUALLY PLAYS OUT**

*Say:*
> "Here's what the cycle looks like in practice.
> They get excited. They start something — a business, a project, a relationship.
> They can work 24 hours straight when they're fascinated.
> Then something gets hard. Or boring. Or doesn't match the fantasy.
> And they stop. They retreat to video games, social media, whatever the escape is.
> Then the itch comes back. New project. New burst of energy.
> Same result.
> And they end up with ten half-finished projects in fifteen different directions.
> None of them complete. None of them real.
> The thing they fear most — wasting their life — is exactly what they're creating."

*On slide:*
- Excitement → start → obstacle or boredom → retreat
- Back to fantasy → new project → same cycle
- 10 steps in 15 directions = nowhere

---

## SLIDE 8 — The Defenses

**THREE WAYS THEY PROTECT THE FANTASY**

*Say:*
> "When you work with these people — and I used to be one of them —
> you start to see the defenses they use to protect the cycle.
> The first is circumstance hunting.
> They believe that in the perfect circumstances, their greatness will emerge.
> So they keep searching for the right job, the right city, the right people.
> The second is blame. And the sneakiest version?
> They blame past them. Not present them. Past them.
> 'If I'd made the right choice back then, I wouldn't be here.'
> But they don't change now. Which tells you it's not actually taking responsibility.
> The third is fragility. Health issues, mental health, past trauma.
> Any of these can be real and genuinely hard.
> But the Puer uses them as a shield.
> Not to solve — to excuse."

*On slide:*
- Circumstance hunting: "I just need the right conditions"
- Blame: past them, not present them
- Fragility: real struggles used as permanent excuses

---

## SLIDE 9 — The Rucksack

**THE HEROIC WORKAROUND**

*Say:*
> "This is my favourite part of the whole concept.
> Von Franz tells this story of a young man — a classic Puer —
> who did serious mountaineering.
> But he hated carrying a rucksack.
> So instead of just... carrying the bag...
> he trained himself through yogic breathing to sleep in the snow.
> He went without food. He built elaborate systems.
> All so he wouldn't have to be a normie with a backpack.
> He would rather be heroically absurd than ordinarily practical.
> And that is the Puer in one image.
> They will do extraordinary, difficult, creative things —
> as long as it means they never have to do the ordinary thing.
> I will be exceptional or I will be nothing."

*On slide:*
- Would rather suffer heroically than be ordinary
- Extraordinary effort to avoid ordinary responsibility
- "I will be exceptional or I will be nothing"

---

## SLIDE 10 — Technology

**MODERN LIFE IS A PERFECT TRAP**

*Say:*
> "Here's why this matters so much right now.
> The Puer Eternus used to be a fraction of the population.
> It is quickly becoming a majority.
> Because everything we've built — video games, social media, pornography, algorithm-driven content —
> is perfectly designed to feed this complex.
> You can be exceptional in a video game. You can live a fantasy relationship online.
> You can consume content that tells you you're meant for greatness
> without ever doing the drab work that greatness actually requires.
> And then there are entire industries built to sell the Puer what they want:
> the promise of extraordinary results without ordinary effort.
> Alpha male gurus. Biohacking. Life optimization. Passive income.
> They're not selling a product. They're selling a fantasy to people who cannot tolerate reality.
> And it works. Because it fits perfectly into the complex."

*On slide:*
- Video games, social media, porn = fantasy on demand
- Entire industries selling: greatness without the grind
- Puer Eternus is becoming the majority

---

## SLIDE 11 — The Cure

**ONE WORD**

*Say:*
> "Jung was asked: what is the cure for the Puer Eternus?
> And he paused. And then he said one word.
> Work.
> And then he hesitated. And he thought about it.
> Is it really as simple as that?
> Yes. It is.
> But not heroic work. Not a 24-hour obsessive sprint.
> Drab work. Boring work. Dishes. Laundry. Showing up on a Tuesday when nothing is exciting.
> The Puer can work incredibly hard. They can do heroic things.
> What they cannot do is work on a dreary rainy morning when it's boring and they have to kick themselves into it.
> That is the one thing they cannot manage.
> And that is the exact thing that builds a life."

*On slide:*
- Jung's cure: one word — Work
- Not heroic work. Drab, ordinary, consistent work
- The thing they cannot do is the exact thing required

---

## SLIDE 12 — The Way Out

**HOW TO ACTUALLY BREAK THE CYCLE**

*Say:*
> "So what do you actually do?
> Three things.
> First — stop looking at the upside. Start looking at the cost.
> The Puer is always scanning for the gain. Eyes on the fantasy, never on the price.
> Flip it. What does this actually cost? What am I actually signing up for?
> Second — sacrifice potential in chunks. Deliberately close doors.
> Make a decision knowing it limits other decisions. That is the point.
> Third — do the small, drab, ordinary things. Consistently. Without making them heroic.
> Clean your room. Not because it's a philosophical act. Because it needs cleaning.
> Cook your food. Do your laundry. Show up.
> Day after day. Without a guarantee that it pays off.
> That is how you grow up."

*On slide:*
- Focus on cost, not gain
- Close doors deliberately — sacrifice potential in chunks
- Small + drab + consistent = a real life

## SLIDE 13 — The Irony

**THE BIG REVEAL**

*Say:*
> "Here's the irony that makes this whole thing so painful.
> The life the Puer actually wants — freedom, impact, a life that matters —
> is only available on the other side of commitment.
> The entrepreneur they dream of being required dropping out of something.
> The relationship they fantasize about requires settling — their most hated word.
> The creative work they imagine requires years of boring craft before it becomes beautiful.
> Everything they want is behind the door they're most afraid to walk through.
> And every year they spend in the boarding area makes it harder.
> Not impossible. But harder.
> So the question isn't whether the door closes.
> It's whether you walk through it yourself or get pushed through by life when you have no choice."

*On slide:*
- The life you want is on the other side of commitment
- Every year in the boarding area = fewer doors
- Walk through it — or get pushed through
