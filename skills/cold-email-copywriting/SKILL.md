# Cold Email Copywriting Skill

This skill encodes the complete outbound copywriting framework from a decade of outbound sales experience and $15M+ generated. Apply it whenever writing or reviewing any outbound message.

The core insight: outbound is convincing a stranger who has zero pre-established trust to take an action. Everything here is designed to build that trust fast, without triggering sales radar.

---

## STEP 0: Before Writing — Clarify the Goal

Every email needs one clear goal. If you can't state it in one sentence, don't write yet.

Pick one:
- **Reply** — they respond yes/no/maybe (lowest ask, highest volume)
- **Watch/click** — they consume an asset (video, report, page)
- **Book a call** — they schedule time with you (bigger ask, higher intent)
- **Buy** — they pay directly (rare for cold outbound)

If the goal is a call, minimize the steps to book it. Include specific times. Don't make them negotiate a calendar.

## THE 4-PART FRAMEWORK

Every outbound message follows this exact sequence:

### Part 1: Personalization
**Goal:** Buy 30 seconds of attention by making them think you wrote this just for them.

This is the highest-ROI part of any email. Drop-off is brutal — if 100% read word one, maybe 50% read past word five. So this sentence has to hook them.

**Rules:**
- Maximum 2 sentences. One is ideal.
- Cannot signal that you are selling something. Zero. If they see a pitch coming, they're gone.
- Use **cold reading** — make an observation that sounds specific but applies broadly. "Love your channel, very no-BS, helped me get started in X" works on almost any content creator or business owner.
- Use **voluntary disclosure** — casually mention something about yourself. People trust you more when you share something unprompted.
- Weave in one real piece of scraped data (college, city, a recent post) to make it feel 100% handcrafted.
- **Never** write AI slop like "Love how passionate you are about process optimization." That's the #1 giveaway. A real person would never say that.

**The test:** Would a friend think this was a personal message? If yes, it works.

**Example that works:**
> "Hey Nick — love the channel, very no-BS. Actually helped me get my footing when I was starting out in consulting. Think I can return the favour."

---

### Part 2: Who Am I
**Goal:** Instantly answer the question forming in their head: "Who is this, and why should I care?"

After a strong personalization, they know you're not a spammer. But now they want to know who you are. Answer it fast — one or two sentences.

The best way: **lead with a client name or result that is in their world.** This does three things at once:
1. Social proof (someone like them already trusts you)
2. Authority (you've produced real results)
3. Shared identity (you're already in their circle)

**Template:**
> "I currently work with [recognizable name or similar company] to help them [do X]. We did [specific number] in [short timeframe]."

Specificity matters enormously. "$112,482 last week" lands ten times harder than "a lot of revenue." Names beat descriptions. Proximity beats distance — reference someone in the same city, niche, or industry.

**What to avoid:** Hedging. "I believe I might be able to help" kills authority instantly. Write like you're confident.

---

### Part 3: The Offer
**Goal:** Make saying no feel irrational.

Two parts — an **observation** and a **too-good-to-be-true offer.**

**The observation:** a specific pain point about their situation. Cold read it — pick a pain that applies to nearly everyone in their category but sounds insightful. "I think you're leaking money through your funnel" works for almost any business with a website.

**The offer formula:**
> "I will do [X result] in [Y timeframe], and if I don't deliver, [risk reversal — you don't pay / full refund / I work free until I do]."

The principle: the prospect risks nothing. All the risk is on you. This kills the last major objection a stranger has.

Good risk reversals:
- "You don't pay a cent unless we hit X"
- "I'll do the first [X] for free so you can see results first"
- "If I don't deliver Y in Z days, full refund"

---

### Part 4: CTA (Call to Action)
**Goal:** Get them to take exactly one specific action.

One action only. Two options = friction = they do nothing.

Keep it as low-effort as possible. The more steps between them and the action, the more they drop off.

- **For a reply:** "Does this make sense? Just reply yes and I'll send it over."
- **For a call:** Give specific times. "I can ring you today at 3pm or tomorrow at 10am — which works?"
- **For an asset:** Lower perceived effort to zero. "Literally just click the link. No download, no form. 90 seconds."

## THE 7 PSYCHOLOGICAL PRINCIPLES

These run underneath every great outbound message. The more you work in naturally, the higher it converts.

| Principle | What it does | How to apply |
|---|---|---|
| **Give First** | Creates obligation before the ask | Offer something genuinely useful upfront — an insight, quick audit, custom loom — before asking for anything |
| **Micro Commitments** | Each small yes makes the next yes easier | Start with a tiny ask (reply yes/no), not the big one (buy my thing). Build escalating steps |
| **Social Proof** | Humans follow consensus | Name specific clients with specific results. Closer to their reference group = more powerful |
| **Authority** | Confidence and credentials lower resistance | Write with certainty. No hedging. Use credentials or recognizable client names |
| **Rapport** | Shared context builds trust | Mirror their tone, industry language, energy. Mention something genuine you share |
| **Scarcity** | Urgency moves decisions | Use real constraints — your capacity, your schedule. Never fake it — people can tell |
| **Shared Identity** | In-group signals unlock trust | Use their language. Reference their world. Make them feel like you're one of them |

## TONE AND FRAME

The frame is **person to person**. Not company to prospect. One human to another.

**The text message test:** If you were typing this to a friend on your phone, would it feel natural? If not, it's too corporate.

**Kill corporate signals:**
- "Hope this finds you well" — delete
- Elaborate signature blocks — strip them
- "We" instead of "I" — change it
- Any sentence that sounds like a brochure — rewrite it

**Write short, casual, slightly imperfect.** Real people don't write perfect emails. Minor imperfections signal authenticity.

**Mirror the reader.** If they're casual, be casual. If formal, adjust. The goal is to feel like you're already in their world.

## DEEKY'S PERSONAL STYLE RULES

These are enforced on every email written for Deeky. Flag any violation before sending.

**Banned words and phrases — flag every one:**
- **"Hey"** — too casual for this market. Use "Hello," or skip the greeting entirely and open straight with the first line.
- **"think"** used as a filler example word — e.g. "think automated inquiry handling, think smarter quoting." Replace with direct description: "automated inquiry handling, smarter quoting."
- **"Worth a look?"** — too soft and ambiguous. The reader doesn't know what action to take. Replace with a direct instruction: "Just reply yes and I'll get it over to you." or "Reply and I'll have something to you in a few days."
- **"Hope this finds you well"** — always delete
- **"I believe" / "I think"** as hedging — kills authority. Cut or rewrite to be direct
- **"I'd love to..."** — too eager, sounds like a pitch

**Greeting rule:**
- Named contact (e.g. Silas, Edwin): "Hi [Name]," or "Hello [Name],"
- General inbox or unknown recipient: skip the greeting entirely — open straight with the first observation line. Confident, not rude.

**Subject line rules (updated from real-world results):**
- In the Zambian/Copperbelt market, professional and capitalized subjects outperform casual lowercase ones
- Proven format: "Website Refresh Proposal for [Company]" — clear, specific, professional. This got AMC Africa to reply within hours.
- Variations that work: "Found a Few Issues on [Company]'s Website", "Quick Thought on [Company]'s Digital Presence"
- Still avoid generic words like "Partnership" or "Opportunity" — keep it specific to their website or digital presence
- Never use CAPS or excessive punctuation

**CTA rule:**
- Never end with a vague question like "Worth a look?" or "Thoughts?"
- Always state the exact action AND what happens next
- Good: "Just reply yes and I'll get the concept over to you."
- Good: "Reply and I'll have something to you within a few days."

## SUBJECT LINES

The subject line gets them to open. That's it.

**What works:**
- Lowercase, casual — "quick thing" or just their first name
- Sounds like a reply to something they forgot about
- Their company name alone as the subject
- A hint at the personalization in the body — intriguing, not salesy

**What kills open rate:**
- CAPS or excessive punctuation
- "Limited time offer!!!"
- Anything that screams mass email
- Benefit-forward ad copy language

## FOLLOW-UPS

Most replies come from follow-ups, not the first send.

- **Follow-up 1 (2-3 days later):** Short bump. "Just checking this didn't get buried."
- **Follow-up 2 (1 week later):** Add new value — a fresh result, a new observation. Not just "following up again."
- **Follow-up 3 (2 weeks later):** The breakup email. "I'll stop bugging you after this — but I really do think there's something here." Often gets the most replies because it creates finality.

## SCORING AN EMAIL (7-Point Check)

When reviewing any outbound message, score each principle yes or no:

1. **Give First** — Does it offer value before asking for anything?
2. **Micro Commitments** — Is the ask small, with natural escalation implied?
3. **Social Proof** — Does it include specific names, numbers, or results?
4. **Authority** — Does it signal confidence without hedging?
5. **Rapport** — Does it feel personal, warm, or tonally matched to the reader?
6. **Scarcity** — Is there any real urgency or capacity constraint mentioned?
7. **Shared Identity** — Does it use their language or establish common ground?

Score out of 7. Below 4 needs a rewrite. Aim for 5+.

## PLATFORM NOTES

- **Email:** Short. Plain text beats HTML. No logo or template.
- **LinkedIn DM:** Even shorter. More conversational. Connection without a note sometimes works better.
- **Instagram DM:** Very casual. Engage with their content first. Voice notes can outperform text.
- **X/Twitter DM:** Short and direct. Match their high-signal communication style.
- **SMS/iMessage:** Super short. First line must hook or it gets deleted.
