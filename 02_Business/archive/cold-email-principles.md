# Cold Email — Principles

Not a fill-in-the-blanks template. These are the principles behind what's working — so every email feels written, not assembled.

Related: [[AMC Africa Client File]] · [[Outreach Tracker]]

---

## What's proven to work (3/3 response rate, Mar–Apr 2026)

The three replies — AMC Africa, NGM Consulting Engineers, Nolands Zambia — all shared the same underlying structure. Not the same words. The same *logic*.

---

## The 7 Principles

### 1. Subject line = their name
Just the company name. Nothing else.

**Why it works:** It triggers pattern recognition, not suspicion. They see their own name in the subject and open it because it looks like it could be important — not like marketing.

**Don't:** "Quick thought for you", "Partnership opportunity", "Re: your website"  
**Do:** `African Mining Consultants` · `NGM Consulting Engineers` · `Nolands Zambia`

---

### 2. Line 1 = proof you looked at them
One sentence. One specific, real detail about the company — a client name, founding year, a specific service, a distinctive fact about how they operate.

**Why it works:** This is what separates you from spam in the first 3 seconds. Generic openers ("I came across your company and was impressed") get deleted. Specific ones get read.

**The rule:** If the line could apply to any company in their sector, rewrite it.  
**Don't:** "You're one of the leading firms in Zambia."  
**Do:** "30 years in, clients like Glencore and First Quantum — impressive track record."  
**Do:** "Wholly Zambian-owned, infrastructure focus, dual offices — you're clearly doing real work."

---

### 3. Social proof = AMC Africa (local, same tier, named)
Mention AMC specifically. Not "a mining company" — *African Mining Consultants*.

**Why it works:** Generic social proof is noise. A named local firm in the same tier as the prospect is credible and specific. They may know AMC. They can look them up. It's not a Silicon Valley case study.

**The frame:** "Most recently African Mining Consultants, who had us rebuild their digital presence from scratch."

---

### 4. The credibility gap diagnosis
"Serious capability, website that doesn't show it." Variations: "The work speaks for itself in person, but online it's a different story." "Serious capability, website that undersells it."

**Why it works:** It's a compliment and a diagnosis at the same time. You're not criticising them — you're recognising that they're better than how they appear online. Every established firm that's too busy to manage their digital presence knows this is true. You're just saying it out loud.

**The psychology:** They feel seen, not attacked. And they have to agree with you to disagree with you.

---

### 5. The AI layer — mention it, don't pitch it
One sentence. Concrete examples, not abstract promises. "Email drafts, follow-ups, document prep, client comms." Not "AI-powered growth solution."

**Why it works:** You're planting the seed, not closing the deal. The AI layer is what differentiates Partnr from a web agency — but trying to explain it fully in a cold email loses the reader. One concrete sentence makes them curious without overwhelming them.

**Don't:** "We leverage cutting-edge artificial intelligence to transform your operations."  
**Do:** "I add a simple AI system that handles the recurring work — email drafts, follow-ups, document prep, client comms."

---

### 6. Zero-risk offer
"Happy to build a quick concept — no cost, no commitment. Just so you can see what it would actually look like."

**Why it works:** You're removing every reason to say no. No money, no time, no obligation. The only thing they have to do is say yes to *looking* at something. The ask is so small it would feel strange to decline.

**The mechanics:** The free concept also forces you to do the work — which means when they say yes, you're ready to show up with something real, not just talk.

---

### 7. Minimum viable close
"Worth a look?" — full stop.

**Why it works:** It's a yes/no question with almost no friction. Compare: "Would you be open to scheduling a 30-minute discovery call to explore potential synergies?" Nobody reads past "would you be open to."

"Worth a look?" is barely even a question. It's an invitation. The answer is almost always yes.

## Follow-up principles

**Day 3 — Short bump.** One line. No new pitch. "Just checking this didn't get buried."

**Day 10 — New value.** One observation about their specific business. Not a repeat of the original email — something new you noticed.

**Day 21 — Breakup.** Leave the door open. "I'll leave you alone after this — but if the timing ever makes sense, I'm easy to find."

**Rule:** Every follow-up should give them a reason to reply, not just remind them you exist.

## When there's no website (or the site is broken)

This is not a problem — it's a stronger hook than a bad website.

**No website at all:**
The gap between their reputation and their online presence is complete. Name it plainly.
> "Looked for [Company Name] online and couldn't find a website. For a firm operating at your level in [city], that's the gap I'd want to close first."

**Broken / expired SSL / site down:**
Even better — you can show you looked, and what you found is itself the proof of the problem.
> "Came across [Company Name] while looking at [sector] firms in [city] — the website is currently down. For a firm doing real work, that's often the first thing a potential client will notice."

**Key principle:** The *absence* of a website is not a reason to skip a prospect. It's the most obvious version of the credibility gap — and the easiest sale to make, because they have no existing investment to defend.

---

## What NOT to do

- Don't explain the AI layer in depth in the cold email — save it for the call
- Don't throw a price when they ask in the first reply — reframe and ask for a call
- Don't use subject lines that sound like marketing: "Quick question", "Partnership opportunity", "Following up"
- Don't make the email long — if it takes more than 45 seconds to read, cut it
- Don't lead with Partnr's features — lead with their situation
