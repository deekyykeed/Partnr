## What you're selling — in order

Don't dump everything at once. The T-shaped approach: go deep on one thing first, expand only when they bite.

**Layer 1 — Website (the door opener)**
This is what he asked for. Show the concept, keep it short. The goal is not to sell the website — it's to open the conversation.

**Layer 2 — Lead generation + ads**
The website alone does nothing if nobody finds it. Once he's nodding at the concept, introduce this: *"The site is step one. The bigger play is driving the right people to it — mining companies, juniors looking for consultants, investors. We run targeted campaigns that make sure AMC shows up when those searches happen."*

**Layer 3 — AI (the real product)**
This is what you actually do. Introduce it as the thing that makes everything else compound. *"Underneath all of this, what I actually engineer is an AI layer — so AMC's team isn't the bottleneck. Enquiries get handled, reports get drafted, ops keep moving when your consultants are on site."*

## Discovery questions — ask these before pitching anything

These are the most important part of the call. Don't skip them to rush to the pitch.

1. **"How do most of AMC's new clients find you currently?"**
   → If the answer is referrals/word of mouth, that's your lead gen opening.

2. **"When a potential client lands on your website, what do you want them to do?"**
   → They probably don't know. That's your conversion gap.

3. **"When your consultants are out on site, what falls through the cracks back at the office?"**
   → This opens the AI conversation naturally.

4. **"What does a typical week look like for you — what takes up most of your time that probably shouldn't?"**
   → This is the operator trap question. Gets them to articulate their own pain.

5. **"Who else at AMC would be involved in a decision like this?"**
   → Critical. Find out if Edwin is the buyer or just the champion.

## The pitch structure

**0–5 min — Rapport + set the agenda**
Don't just dive in. Brief small talk, then take control of the agenda:
*"Edwin, thanks for making time. My plan is: I'll show you the concept in about 5 minutes, then I want to understand AMC a bit better before I say anything else. Sound good?"*

Setting the agenda stops the call from being just a website viewing.

**5–10 min — Show the concept**
Walk him through it. Don't over-explain. Watch his reaction. Ask: *"What stands out to you — good or bad?"*

**10–18 min — Discovery**
Ask the questions above. Listen more than you talk. Every answer is ammunition.

**18–25 min — Expand the offer**
Based on what he tells you, introduce Layer 2 and/or Layer 3. Lead with the one that connects most directly to the pain he described.

*"Based on what you've just told me, the website is probably the least of it — the bigger issue sounds like [X]. Here's how I'd approach that..."*

**25–30 min — The close**
Don't try to close a contract on this call. Close the next step.

*"What I'd like to do is a proper diagnostic on AMC — map out exactly where the gaps are and build a proposal specific to you. That's a 2–3 hour session, either in person or on a call. Are you the right person to take that forward, or do we need anyone else in the room?"*

## Pricing — don't lead with it, but be ready

If he asks what it costs, don't dodge it. Give a range that anchors high:

- **Website only:** $2,000–$5,000 depending on scope
- **Website + lead gen setup:** $5,000–$8,000 + monthly retainer for ads management
- **Full engagement (website + lead gen + AI layer):** $10,000–$15,000 setup, then ongoing retainer

Frame it as investment, not cost: *"For a firm that charges what AMC charges per engagement, if the site brings in one additional client a year, it pays for itself many times over."*

## Objections and how to handle them

**"We don't really need a new website."**
*"Fair enough — the website is honestly just how I started the conversation. What I'm more interested in is whether AMC has a gap between the quality of work you do and how visible that work is to the right clients. That's the actual problem I solve."*

**"What have you done for other companies?"**
*"AMC is actually my first formal engagement through Partnr — I've been building these systems inside my own business for the past two years. I'm selective about who I work with, which is why I came to you. I'd rather show you what I can do than talk about it."* (Honest, confident, not defensive.)

**"Send me a proposal."**
*"I can do that — but I'd rather it be accurate to AMC specifically. Can I ask you two or three more questions before I put it together, so it actually reflects what you need rather than a generic document?"*

**"We need to involve [other person]."**
*"Perfect — that's exactly what I was going to suggest. When's a good time to get the three of us in a room?"*
